A1 - Interactive Experience Research Assignment

Analysis of RichBrown's Portfolio

s4081354, Marco J. Sihombing

1. What was the first thing you paid attention to when interacting with the experience?

The first element that really caught my attention as soon as the page finished loading was the high-quality video shots of what I quickly assumed correctly to be the Designer, Rich Brown himself, in a brutalist warehouse interior setting, looking very sharp and concentrated, with simple attire and interesting shots really radiate his mindful impression.

2. Spend two minutes with the experience and create a list of each of your discrete actions.

  • Wiggling the mouse to play with the custom pencil toy cursor
  • Pressing on random elements such as the video, texts, and the big “B” and “R” letters on the left
  • Pressed the “+” button hoping to find his work but only his contact details
  • Scrolling M3, eager to find a showreel or at least an instance of his work

3. What part of the experience did you spend the most time engaging with

Most of my time in this experience was spent observing his work while engaging with different ways of viewing it through various animations. For example, when scrolling through his six signature displayed works (Bathmate, Villainy, Intentional Spaces Summit, Green Room, Your Brain On Art, Ivy Park), the horizontal scrolling of the panels is always smooth and fluid. Additionally, hovering over each panel causes it to move vertically, making the experience feel interactive and giving users a sense of control over how they explore his portfolio. This design choice effectively sparks curiosity and encourages users to click on the panels to learn more.

4. What was the most common action in your two-minute interaction with the experience?

Sometimes, I intentionally step away from the keyboard after hovering or clicking on something to see how the site reacts. From there, I observe from left to right, noticing any differences and gaining a deeper appreciation for the ‘idle’ animations or movements the designer has incorporated. For example, the looped rotating text in the top right that says 'Good Shit' ensures there's always something to look at. Additionally, the panels themselves function as videos, reinforcing the intent for users to focus more on visuals rather than reading.

5. What is your impression of the intended primary goal of the interactive experience?

With it being a portfolio of a somewhat renowned User Experience designer, it is clear that the site is designed to draw us in through eye-catching visual movements and interactive elements, mainly using our scroll wheel. Another thing is that his site is so hard to read. It’s as if he does not want us to read any of the texts, just to focus more on the visuals. His goal is for us to explore the site, hovering over different parts to discover hidden details, scrolling to reveal more, and engaging with the visuals playfully. Throughout this process, the designer seamlessly showcases his work and achievements in an immersive way that makes us want to keep exploring while ensuring the experience remains informative, engaging, and non-repetitive to look at.

6. How does the interactive experience communicate this primary goal?

The interactive experience effectively communicates its goal by using motion, responsiveness, and intuitive interactions to guide the user engagement through this experience. For example, you will never find his work without scrolling; that sense of curiosity makes us want to find his actual works after getting the good first impression on his stunning welcome page, complimenting the web over an existing compliment/praise. The scroll-based animations direct attention to new elements popping up every second as we play with it, making the experience feel intentional and effortless.

Furthermore, hover effects introduce an element of discovery, subtly rewarding users for exploring and reinforcing the idea that interaction is key to uncovering the designer’s work. The pacing of these interactions ensures that users remain engaged without feeling overwhelmed, keeping the experience immersive while maintaining clarity and focus on the designer’s achievements.

7. What is your impression of how the experience should be interacted with over time? (For how long and how many different times)

Well, considering stuff about session lengths, repeat visits, and inveractivity’s role. I would say that the experience as a whole was designed for BOTH short and extended interactions. On the first visit and glance on the page, I think most users would spend a few minutes exploring the smooth movements and interactive elements, as in engaging with different panels and texts to uncover hidden details and movements. However, the eye-straining texts, dynamic design, idle animations, and subtle interactions encourage users to revisit either out of curiosity to explore more in deeper depth.

8. How does the interactive experience communicate how it should be interacted with over time?

The interactive experience communicates how it should be engaged with over time through its use of smooth scrolling and fluid transitions that create a sense of a somewhat immediate flow. Moreover, it encourages the users to explore at their own pace and progress. Over time, the use of idle animations, looping texts, and hover effects concludes that this experience isn’t meant to be rushed because it all unfolds gradually.

9. What other media forms (digital or otherwise) does the experience reference?

The experience pulls from a bunch of different media influences, both digital and physical, which makes sense given the designer’s background. From what I found, he was influenced by illustrations, architecture drawings, retro tech, and computers growing up, and you can really see that reflected in the site’s design. It feels like a mix of a showreel and an interactive exhibition, where you’re meant to sit back and take in the visuals rather than actively searching for content. The smooth animations and transitions give it a cinematic vibe, almost like a motion graphics piece that unfolds as you scroll. The hover effects and scrolling mechanics feel a lot like video game interfaces, where you’re rewarded for exploring. Plus, the bold visuals and dynamic text remind me of modern advertising or magazine layouts, grabbing attention while keeping the experience smooth and engaging.

10. What does this reference/s communicate to you about how you should act when engaging with your research experience?

Just sit back and let the website do the work—it’s designed to be an immersive experience without needing much effort to explore. It flows naturally, making things easier for both us as users and the UX designer’s vision. It’s kind of like watching a movie or a showreel, where everything is presented smoothly, so you can just take it in without overthinking.

11. What does this reference/s communicate to you about how you should feel when engaging with your research experience?

Similarly said, I think we should have a sense and feeling of being laid-back and at ease as we go through different elements of the site. As we go through those panels and works, users will be able to fully analyze and admire the different pieces in his design portfolio without focusing too much on the animations or transitions surrounding them. Moreover, the website design wants us to take our time with no intention of quickly skimming through them.

12. What is the most frustrating part of the interaction to you and what makes that part frustrating?

In general, the most frustrating part of the experience for me is how some panels for a single project are spread out and not placed close together. Additionally, the varying panel sizes, while visually striking, force the viewer’s eyes to constantly move from top to bottom and left to right just to grasp the full context and art style of a particular piece.

Beyond that, the site deliberately makes text and images pop out with an almost ‘in-your-face’ approach. While this bold design choice is attention-grabbing, it can also be quite eye-straining and even frustrating at times. It almost feels like being bombarded with advertisement pop-ups as I scroll through his list of other works, especially with the large, all-caps white text (excluding his six signature projects).

13. What is the most satisfying part of the interaction to you and what makes that part satisfying?

After playing and exploring the website countless times for maybe more than a handful of hours, I would have to say the custom animated mouse cursor has easily entertained me the most out of all things. It’s never-ending and always a treat to wiggle your mouse before scrolling or clicking into a panel.
